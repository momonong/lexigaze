## 1. Executive Summary
This experiment marks a turning point for the LexiGaze project. By transitioning from point-based calibration (Bayesian Snap) to sequence-based decoding (Spatio-Temporal Viterbi), we successfully mitigated extreme systematic hardware drift (+45px vertical). The accuracy jumped from a baseline of **16.7%** to **48.1%**, proving that reading dynamics (saccades and regressions) are more powerful priors than static word difficulty alone.

## 2. Methodology

### A. The Viterbi Integration
1.  **State Space**: Hidden states are the words in the GECO sentence.
2.  **Transitions (Skill 2)**: An $N \times N$ matrix modeling saccadic momentum, word skipping (low CM), and regressions (high CM/L2 behavior).
3.  **Emissions (Skill 1)**: A dynamic cognitive field where word "gravity" decays as exposure increases, preventing the decoder from getting stuck on a single high-mass word.
4.  **Decoding (Skill 3)**: Viterbi algorithm with banded optimization ($i \pm 3$) to find the most likely global reading path.

### B. Hardware Stress Test
- **Systematic Drift**: +45px (Vertical).
- **Jitter**: $\sigma_x=40, \sigma_y=30$.
- **Dataset**: GECO Subject `pp01`, Trial 5.

## 4. Technical Analysis: Why it works
- **Sequence over Points**: The Static Bayesian model treats every gaze point as an independent event. In contrast, the Viterbi decoder "knows" that a reader is unlikely to jump randomly across the screen. 
- **The Momentum Effect**: Even when a specific word has low Cognitive Mass, the "momentum" from the previous word (Saccadic Prior) pulls the decoder forward.
- **Exposure Decay**: The dynamic field (Skill 1) ensures that once a word is "read," its probability drops, forcing the decoder to look for the next logical word in the sequence.
